# Color Interaction Rules

## Purpose

This file defines how fluorescent colors should interact inside the vintage 1970s–1980s
blacklight poster aesthetic.

This is not a general color theory file.
It is specifically about retinal punch, blacklight behavior, glow hierarchy, and keeping the image
from turning into fluorescent soup.

## Core Principle

Colors do not merely describe surfaces.
They behave like energized pigment against darkness.

Black is the field.
Color is the event.

## Main Roles

### 1. Dominant Color
The main emotional driver of the piece.

Common dominant colors:
- electric blue
- hot magenta
- ultraviolet purple

- acid green
- hot orange

### 2. Support Color
Separates forms, adds gradient motion, and enriches depth.

Common support colors:
- cyan
- violet
- blue
- green
- orange

### 3. Accent Color
Small-area retinal shock.

Common accents:
- white
- pale cyan
- radioactive yellow
- hot pink
- orange sparks

## Best Blacklight Pairings

### Electric Blue + Magenta
Effect:
- classic cosmic fluorescence
- dreamy and high-energy
- excellent for wolves, women, moons, fantasy icons

Use:
- blue as dominant
- magenta as glow transition or support
- white or cyan as accent

### Violet + Cyan
Effect:
- lunar, mystical, cool-toned, elegant
- great for celestial feminine subjects and moon scenes

Use:
- violet as dominant
- cyan as edge energy

- white sparingly for peak highlights

### Acid Green + Electric Blue
Effect:
- toxic, alien, biofluorescent
- good for mushrooms, portals, spores, snakes, occult glow objects

Use:
- blue as grounding hue
- green as active energy
- yellow only in tiny doses

### Hot Pink + Orange
Effect:
- aggressive heat, fantasy glamour, solar bloom
- good for mushrooms, feminine glamour posters, fantasy beasts

Use:
- pink as dominant or support
- orange as heat accent
- purple shadow for control

### Blue + Cyan + White
Effect:
- moonlit, icy, sacred, spectral
- useful for mystical animals, owls, moons, crystals

Use:
- blue field
- cyan halo
- white only for sharp energy points

## Safe Clash Recipes

### Recipe: Ultraviolet Dream
- dominant: violet
- support: magenta
- support: electric blue
- accent: cyan-white

### Recipe: Toxic Portal
- dominant: electric blue
- support: acid green
- accent: yellow-green

- tiny orange spark if needed

### Recipe: Solar Fungi
- dominant: hot pink
- support: orange
- support: yellow
- shadow support: electric blue or purple

### Recipe: Lunar Sorceress
- dominant: deep blue-violet
- support: cyan
- support: magenta
- accent: white

## White Usage Rules

White is not filler.
White is ionized emphasis.

Use white for:
- eye glints
- crystal tips
- star points
- hottest emission cores
- moon-edge micro-highlights

Avoid:
- large white fills
- flat white backgrounds inside the poster logic
- equal white distribution across the whole image

Too much white kills the fluorescence illusion by flattening contrast.

## Yellow Usage Rules

Yellow is dangerous because it screams loud as hell.

Use yellow for:
- tiny accent sparks
- moon-hot highlights
- mushroom edge energy
- electric support in green-heavy schemes

Avoid:

- covering large zones in flat yellow
- mixing equal yellow with all other hues
- turning the whole image into a carnival air horn

## Green Usage Rules

Acid green is extremely powerful and can hijack the whole palette.

Use green as:
- toxic accent
- spore field energy
- occult plasma
- edge contamination
- bioluminescent insert

Avoid:
- pairing equal green with equal magenta unless deliberately going chaotic
- using green everywhere in portrait subjects unless you want alien drift

## Orange Usage Rules

Orange provides heat and theatricality.

Use orange for:
- mushroom cap heat
- tiger stripes
- solar highlights
- fantasy beast accents
- fiery support in pink/purple palettes

Orange works especially well when controlled by purple or blue shadows.

## Mud Risks

These combinations can collapse or weaken the style if mishandled:

### Green + Red
Risk:
- turns muddy or holiday-coded unless stylized aggressively

### Too Many Mid-Saturation Blends
Risk:
- destroys retinal punch
- feels dusty instead of fluorescent

### Equal-Area Rainbow
Risk:
- no hierarchy
- no focal structure
- becomes toy-like or generic psychedelic sludge

## Contrast Rules

1. One dominant hue family.
2. One support hue family.
3. One accent family.
4. Black must separate major glow zones.
5. White must remain rare.
6. Warm/cool clashes should be intentional, not evenly distributed.

## Value Rules

Even in a saturated image, value hierarchy still matters.

Recommended structure:
- large black or near-black field
- medium-bright support zones
- bright focal region
- tiny extreme highlights

## Good Subject-Palette Pairing Examples

### Wolf
- electric blue
- violet
- cyan
- white accents

### Tiger
- orange
- yellow
- purple shadow
- cyan or green micro-accents

### Mushroom Cluster
- hot pink
- orange
- acid green

- electric blue shadow

### Cosmic Sorceress
- magenta
- violet
- cyan
- white eye/light accents

### Owl
- blue
- cyan
- white
- violet shadow

## Final Rule

The palette should feel like a fluorescent event staged against a velvet abyss.
If all colors shout equally, the poster loses its spell.

# Shape Language

## Purpose

This file defines the dominant shape tendencies of the vintage blacklight poster style.

This style is not only about glow and color.
It also depends on recurring forms that support aura, symbolism, and poster readability.

## Core Shape Principle

Shapes should feel iconic, soft-edged, mystical, decorative, and emotionally legible from a
distance.

The language favors:
- rounded forms
- halo geometry
- plume-like curves
- aura ovals
- radiating bursts

- crescent arcs
- tapered flame and smoke shapes

It generally avoids:
- rigid industrial geometry
- clean UI boxes
- hard technical grids
- aggressive angular abstraction unless hybridized intentionally

## Major Shape Families

### 1. Halo Circles
Role:
- sacred emphasis
- framing
- lunar reference
- aura amplification

Examples:
- full moons
- circular aura rings
- concentric glow orbits
- halo discs behind heads

### 2. Crescent Arcs
Role:
- moon symbolism
- dynamic framing
- elegant celestial rhythm

Examples:
- crescent moons
- curved horn-like silhouettes
- half-rings around faces or mushrooms

### 3. Feathered Radial Bursts
Role:
- energy projection
- decorative emphasis
- mystical charge

Examples:
- starburst halos
- soft radial aura lines

- plume-like radiating glow fields

### 4. Oval Aura Fields
Role:
- body or face framing
- compositional grouping
- focal containment

Examples:
- glowing oval around a face
- mushroom cluster enclosed in a soft aura shape
- animal head framed by mist oval

### 5. Flame / Smoke Tendrils
Role:
- motion suggestion
- atmosphere
- fantasy embellishment

Examples:
- drifting smoke ribbons
- magical vapor curls
- aura flames around symbols

### 6. Mushroom Domes and Rounded Caps
Role:
- biomorphic fantasy forms
- glow carriers
- repeating visual rhythm

Examples:
- mushroom caps
- rounded cloud humps
- glowing dome forms in background motifs

### 7. Cat-Eye Diagonals
Role:
- glamour, danger, mystique
- facial emphasis
- predatory energy

Examples:
- feline eyes
- slanted eyebrow arcs

- pointed highlights within otherwise soft forms

### 8. Soft Triangular Silhouettes
Role:
- mountain forms
- crystals
- fantasy structures
- compositional anchoring

Examples:
- simplified glowing mountains
- crystal points
- wizard-tower silhouettes
- portal peaks

## Subject Shape Tendencies

### Wolves / Big Cats
- dominant face oval
- triangular ears
- tapered eye shapes
- radiating fur-edge silhouettes
- moon-circle support

### Celestial Feminine
- oval face
- flowing hair plumes
- crescent or halo framing
- soft shoulder curves
- decorative orbit shapes

### Mushrooms
- cap domes
- pillar stems
- clustered rounded repetition
- spore dots
- portal circles behind cluster

### Occult Symbol Assemblies
- circles
- radiating spokes
- symmetrical ovals
- crystal triangles
- eye-shaped centers

## Composition Shape Logic

Good compositions often combine:
- one dominant circular or oval framing shape
- one major subject silhouette
- several soft support arcs or plume curves
- sparse sharp accents

This keeps the image readable while still decorative.

## Sharp vs Soft Distribution

Recommended:
- 70–85% soft and rounded forms
- 15–30% sharp accents

Sharp accents should land in:
- eyes
- crystal tips
- stars
- fangs
- tiny symbolic details

## Drift Warnings

### Too Many Hard Geometric Forms
Result:
- drifts into synthwave or generic digital poster territory

### Too Much Perfect Symmetry
Result:
- drifts into lifeless sacred geometry sludge

### Too Many Tiny Repeating Shapes
Result:
- destroys poster read
- feels noisy instead of iconic

## Final Rule

The shape language should feel like mystical glow made visible:
rounded, radiating, symbolic, and slightly sleazy in the best possible way.

# Edge Behavior

## Purpose

This file defines how edges should behave in the vintage blacklight poster style.

Edge treatment is one of the main things that separates this style from generic neon, flat poster
art, or digital sludge.

## Core Principle

Edges should not all behave the same way.

The style depends on an edge hierarchy:
- some edges glow sharply
- some diffuse softly
- some dissolve into haze
- some pop with crisp accent highlights

If every edge is equally hard, the image becomes stiff.
If every edge is equally soft, the image melts into useless soup.

## Main Edge Types

### 1. Silhouette Glow Edge
Role:
- separate subject from black field
- create poster readability
- intensify blacklight emission illusion

Traits:
- medium to bright
- often colored, not pure white
- slightly feathered outward
- strongest around main subject

Use for:
- animal outlines
- face contours

- mushroom caps
- moon rims
- crystal edges

### 2. Soft Inner Gradient Edge
Role:
- shape internal forms without harsh realism
- preserve airbrush feel

Traits:
- low contrast
- feathered
- smooth transition
- often chromatic rather than neutral

Use for:
- cheeks
- fur transitions
- soft shadows
- internal mushroom color changes
- aura transitions

### 3. Accent Hard Edge
Role:
- create local snap and focal emphasis

Traits:
- small area only
- high contrast
- often white, cyan, or hot yellow
- sharp compared to surrounding softness

Use for:
- eye highlights
- fang glints
- crystal tips
- star points
- moon edge sparks

### 4. Dissolving Atmospheric Edge
Role:
- blend subject or glow into surrounding haze
- create mysticism and softness

Traits:
- low-definition boundary
- airy fade
- sometimes broken or patchy
- can merge with smoke or aura field

Use for:
- hair ends
- smoke ribbons
- portal haze
- cloud edges
- outer bloom regions

### 5. Double Edge Glow
Role:
- amplify electric or fluorescent feel

Traits:
- inner bright edge + outer soft halo
- very useful for blacklight look
- must be controlled to avoid looking cheesy in the wrong way

Use for:
- moon rims
- animal fur outlines
- mushroom caps
- magical symbols

## Edge Hierarchy Rules

1. Main subject silhouette gets the clearest edge treatment.
2. Secondary subjects get softer or dimmer edges.
3. Decorative elements should not compete with the focal edge.
4. Hard edges should be rare and strategic.
5. Outer-field atmosphere should often dissolve rather than hard-stop.

## Edge Distribution by Subject Type

### Animal Posters
- strong face silhouette edge
- glowing eye accents
- fur edges broken into soft feathered light

### Celestial Feminine Posters

- soft facial edges
- stronger hair halo edge
- sharp eye/lip/jewelry or star accents only

### Mushroom Posters
- bright cap rims
- soft stem gradients
- dissolved spore haze
- selective hard sparkle accents

### Symbolic Occult Posters
- sharper icon contours
- concentric glow edge logic
- controlled aura diffusion

## Overspray Logic

Airbrush softness can be simulated by slightly diffusing selected bright edges outward into
black.

Best uses:
- moon halos
- shoulder glow
- mushroom cap bloom
- wolf fur fringe
- aura ornaments

Do not overspray everything.
Too much kills structure.

## Edge Failure Modes

### All Hard
Result:
- looks like vector neon sticker art
- loses analog fantasy softness

### All Soft
Result:
- no poster readability
- no focal hook

### White Everywhere
Result:

- cheapens palette
- destroys chromatic hierarchy

### No Silhouette Separation
Result:
- subject sinks into black instead of emerging from it

## Final Rule

The best edge behavior feels like fluorescent pigment trying to escape the form without fully
leaving it.

# Prompt Knobs

## Purpose

This file defines adjustable control dimensions for prompting within the vintage blacklight poster
style.

These knobs can be used in:
- manual prompting
- agent workflows
- prompt builders
- UI sliders
- combinatorial generation systems

## Main Knobs

### 1. Darkness Level
Controls how much black or near-black dominates the composition.

Low:
- more visible environment
- more atmospheric fill
- less classic blacklight force

Medium:
- balanced black field with visible support motifs

High:
- near-total black dominance
- strongest fluorescent punch
- most classic poster feel

Prompt language:
- deep black velvet background
- near-total black void
- heavily black-dominant composition
- sparse luminous content against darkness

### 2. Glow Aggression
Controls how intense the glow system behaves.

Low:
- gentle aura
- moonlit or dreamy

Medium:
- clear blacklight energy
- good poster readability

High:
- strong rim emission
- strong halo fields
- more theatrical and loud

Prompt language:
- soft fluorescent bloom
- pronounced rim glow
- aggressive ultraviolet emission
- intense spectral halo

### 3. Softness
Controls airbrush quality vs crispness.

Low:
- more graphic and sharp
- risks digital drift

Medium:
- classic painterly airbrush

High:

- dreamy, hazy, soft-focus, plush

Prompt language:
- airbrushed softness
- feathered glow transitions
- dreamlike haze
- velvety bloom

### 4. Mystical Density
Controls symbolic and spiritual embellishment.

Low:
- cleaner poster icon
- fewer symbols
- simpler focus

Medium:
- stars, moon, aura, crystals, smoke

High:
- richly symbolic
- occult or cosmic layering
- more decorative charge

Prompt language:
- celestial framing motifs
- mystical aura symbols
- occult decorative atmosphere
- cosmic spiritual embellishment

### 5. Decorative Density
Controls the amount of stars, sparkles, smoke, rings, and support motifs.

Low:
- cleaner iconic read

Medium:
- classic poster richness

High:
- ornate head-shop maximalism
- greater risk of clutter

Prompt language:

- sparse stars
- decorative aura haze
- richly embellished cosmic background
- ornate mystical framing

### 6. Subject Dominance
Controls how large and central the subject feels.

Low:
- more scene-based
- more environmental storytelling

Medium:
- subject-centered with supporting environment

High:
- giant central icon
- strongest across-room read

Prompt language:
- large central figure
- iconic centered subject
- dominant face-forward composition
- poster-scale focal subject

### 7. Era Bias
Controls whether the piece leans more late-70s or more 80s.

#### 70s Bias
Traits:
- softer
- more mystical
- more earthy-occult fantasy
- more painterly and dreamy

Prompt language:
- softer 1970s blacklight poster feel
- mystical head-shop wall poster vibe
- dreamier, more analog, more spiritual

#### 80s Bias
Traits:
- more electric
- cleaner transitions

- stronger cyan-magenta energy
- more fantasy paperback adjacency

Prompt language:
- electric 1980s blacklight fantasy poster
- cleaner airbrush glow
- neon-charged but still painterly

### 8. Palette Heat
Controls whether the piece leans cool, warm, or mixed.

Cool:
- blue, cyan, violet, white
- lunar, spectral, icy

Warm:
- pink, orange, yellow, purple
- hot, glam, solar, fungal

Mixed:
- strongest theatrical contrast
- highest drama
- higher risk if uncontrolled

### 9. Symbolic Bias
Controls the motif family.

Options:
- cosmic
- occult
- animal
- celestial feminine
- mushroom / forest dream
- fantasy beast
- crystal / sigil

### 10. Symmetry Bias
Controls how balanced the image feels.

Low:
- more organic and scene-like

Medium:
- balanced but alive

High:
- totemic, icon-like, ritualistic
- must be controlled to avoid sacred geometry sludge

## Recommended Default Settings

For a classic blacklight poster:
- darkness: high
- glow aggression: medium-high
- softness: medium-high
- mystical density: medium
- decorative density: medium
- subject dominance: high
- era bias: hybrid 70s/80s
- palette heat: mixed
- symmetry: medium

## Example Control String

High darkness, medium-high glow aggression, high subject dominance, medium mystical
density, medium decorative density, soft airbrushed rendering, hybrid late-70s to 80s blacklight
poster energy, cool-dominant palette with magenta accents.

## Final Rule

Do not turn every knob to maximum unless you actively want a glorious glowing mess.
The best results come from deliberate imbalance.

# Mask Logic

## Purpose

This file defines useful render masks for translating the vintage blacklight poster style into
shaders, compositing graphs, render passes, or node-based systems.

The goal is to preserve glow hierarchy and poster readability.

## Core Principle

Do not treat the whole image as one giant glowing blob.
Break the image into meaningful mask layers.

## Main Masks

### 1. Core Silhouette Mask
Purpose:
- defines the main subject body or dominant icon
- preserves readability
- acts as the anchor for all other effects

Examples:
- wolf head
- woman face
- mushroom cluster
- crystal sigil
- moon disc

Recommended behavior:
- least blurred
- highest compositional priority
- cleanest value separation

### 2. Emissive Edge Mask
Purpose:
- controls local rim glow
- separates subject from black field

Sources:
- silhouette boundaries
- selected feature contours
- cap edges
- fur tips
- moon rims
- crystal edges

Recommended behavior:
- medium blur or feather
- high color intensity
- stronger near focal regions

### 3. Internal Radiance Mask
Purpose:
- creates glow from inside forms rather than only around them

Examples:
- eyes
- crystal cores
- mushroom gills
- magical symbols
- moon centers

Recommended behavior:
- higher brightness
- tighter mask than halo field
- often combined with selective bloom

### 4. Bloom Halo Mask
Purpose:
- expands bright areas outward into soft visible luminosity

Sources:
- strongest emissive areas
- focal symbols
- faces
- moons
- aura rings

Recommended behavior:
- broad blur
- low to medium opacity
- should not flood the entire composition

### 5. Aura Field Mask
Purpose:
- creates the larger mystical atmosphere around the main event

Examples:
- fog oval around subject
- diffuse portal field
- chromatic spiritual haze
- dream mist behind hair or mushrooms

Recommended behavior:
- very soft
- patchy or irregular
- not perfectly uniform
- low intensity

### 6. Accent Highlight Mask
Purpose:
- allows sharp contrast pops

Examples:
- eye glints
- star points
- fang tips
- crystal tips
- metallic or wet-looking points

Recommended behavior:
- tiny area only
- highest brightness
- often white or pale cyan
- minimal blur

### 7. Atmosphere / Smoke Mask
Purpose:
- controls drifting smoke, haze, soft vapor, and background fog

Recommended behavior:
- low-frequency motion if animated
- feathered edges
- partial transparency
- should support, not overpower

### 8. Star / Spark Mask
Purpose:
- distributes sparse decorative sparkle across outer field or focal region

Recommended behavior:
- stochastic placement
- low density in general
- slightly denser near focal zone if desired
- occasional twinkle or intensity modulation

### 9. Background Preservation Mask
Purpose:
- protects pure black or near-black areas from being destroyed by over-blooming

Recommended behavior:
- keep large darkness reserves
- essential for style integrity
- use to clamp haze spread and glow contamination

## Optional Advanced Masks

### 10. Color Region Mask
Purpose:
- assign different palette ramps to different subject zones

Examples:
- blue fur edges
- magenta shadow bloom
- cyan eye glow
- orange mushroom cap heat

### 11. Symmetry Bias Mask
Purpose:

- enforce or soften partial symmetry in totemic compositions

### 12. Decorative Ornament Mask
Purpose:
- control concentric rings, zodiac fragments, sigils, and border elements

## Recommended Layer Order

1. background preservation
2. core silhouette
3. internal radiance
4. emissive edge
5. bloom halo
6. aura field
7. atmosphere/smoke
8. star/spark
9. accent highlights

## Failure Warnings

### No Background Preservation
Result:
- everything gets fogged
- blacklight punch dies

### Bloom Halo Too Broad
Result:
- subject loses edge structure
- looks smeared

### Accent Highlight Mask Too Large
Result:
- white dominates
- palette gets flattened

### Aura Field Too Uniform
Result:
- fake digital haze
- loses mystical irregularity

## Final Rule

The masks should make the image feel like a glowing icon emerging from black velvet, not like
a full-screen neon accident.

# Hybridization Recipes

## Purpose

This file explains how to hybridize the vintage blacklight poster style with adjacent aesthetics
without blowing up its core identity.

## Core Rule

When hybridizing, preserve these blacklight poster anchors:
- black dominance
- fluorescent palette logic
- glow hierarchy
- iconic subject readability
- mystical / fantasy wall-poster energy

If those die, the hybrid is no longer really blacklight poster style.

---

## 1. Blacklight Poster + Fantasy Paperback Cover

### Keep
- black void field
- fluorescent palette
- moon halos
- magical aura logic

### Borrow
- stronger creature rendering
- more fantasy costume detail
- slightly more environment structure
- epic mythic narrative hints

### Use For
- dragons
- unicorns
- sorceresses
- spectral horses

- castle skies

### Watch Out
Too much scene complexity will kill poster readability.

---

## 2. Blacklight Poster + Velvet Painting

### Keep
- blacklight fluorescence
- poster icon readability
- moonlit aura

### Borrow
- plush darkness
- lush softness
- kitschy sacred-animal energy
- tactile depth cues

### Use For
- wolves
- tigers
- panthers
- waterfalls
- moon mountains

### Watch Out
Do not let the texture overwhelm the glow hierarchy.

---

## 3. Blacklight Poster + Occult Engraving

### Keep
- black field
- emission behavior
- mystical icon dominance

### Borrow
- symbolic arrangement
- concentric ritual geometry
- sigils and engraved line motifs
- altar-like composition structure

### Use For
- radiant eyes
- zodiac wheels
- crystal altars
- ceremonial portraits

### Watch Out
This combo can drift into generic sacred geometry sludge if the glow and focal hierarchy get
weak.

---

## 4. Blacklight Poster + Van Art Airbrush

### Keep
- blacklight fluorescence
- fantasy mysticism
- central iconic subject

### Borrow
- hotter chrome-like accents
- more dramatic horizon lines
- stronger 80s electric edge treatment
- mural-like theatricality

### Use For
- tigers
- eagles
- fantasy women
- dragons
- cosmic road-trip energy

### Watch Out
Too much chrome or sunset geometry pushes it into straight retro airbrush instead of blacklight
poster.

---

## 5. Blacklight Poster + Slime Mold Bio-Glow

### Keep
- black field
- aura-driven lighting

- poster-scale iconic subject

### Borrow
- branching bio-patterns
- wet luminous textures
- toxic greens and electric yellows
- spore and growth logic

### Use For
- mushrooms
- occult portals
- alien flora
- fungal godfaces
- bioluminescent landscapes

### Watch Out
Control the green or it will hijack the whole damn palette.

---

## 6. Blacklight Poster + Fractal Optics

### Keep
- subject-centered poster readability
- black dominance
- fluorescent color hierarchy

### Borrow
- recursive edge echoes
- chromatic interference bands
- aura duplication
- radiating optical distortions

### Use For
- moon maidens
- wolves
- glowing mushrooms
- radiant symbols
- psychedelic fantasy beasts

### Watch Out
Do not let the fractal logic replace the main icon.
The image still needs to read as poster first, weirdness second.

---

## 7. Blacklight Poster + New Age Glam

### Keep
- ultraviolet dream palette
- aura framing
- dreamy black void
- celestial decorative motifs

### Borrow
- glam face lighting
- glossy hair halos
- more decorative luxury sparkle logic
- feminine sacred excess

### Use For
- moon maidens
- cosmic sorceresses
- celestial glamour portraits
- goddess posters

### Watch Out
Too much gloss and cuteness can weaken the blacklight grit and sleaze-mystic edge.

---

## 8. Blacklight Poster + Mushroom Visionary Art

### Keep
- poster-scale subject clarity
- darkness structure
- glow hierarchy

### Borrow
- spores
- fungal domes
- visionary portals
- psychotropic landscape logic

### Use For
- giant mushrooms
- forest temples
- fungal portals

- mycelial altars
- dream ecology posters

### Watch Out
Do not let the whole composition turn into undifferentiated rainbow fungus mush.

---

## Recommended Hybrid Strength

Use:
- 70% blacklight poster core
- 30% borrowed style

If you flip that ratio, you usually lose the identity.

## Final Rule

Hybridize like a necromancer, not a blender.
Steal the useful organs. Do not liquefy the corpse.

# Motion Behavior

## Purpose

This file defines how the vintage 1970s–1980s blacklight poster style should move when
translated into animation, shaders, motion graphics, or interactive systems.

The style should not move like a slick modern UI, a hyperactive EDM visualizer, or a chaotic
particle disaster.
It should move like a glowing dream object that is alive in the dark.

## Core Motion Principle

Motion should feel:
- slow to medium
- hypnotic
- aura-driven
- atmospheric
- slightly ritualistic
- emotionally legible

Good motion makes the poster feel haunted, alive, sacred, dreamy, or theatrically charged.

## Main Motion Families

### 1. Aura Breathing
Description:
A slow expansion and contraction of glow around the main subject.

Best for:
- moon maidens
- wolves
- crystals
- moons
- mushrooms
- radiant symbols

Behavior:
- low frequency
- low amplitude
- gentle edge expansion
- soft brightness modulation

Avoid:
- fast pulsing
- hard strobe behavior

### 2. Mist Drift
Description:
Slow atmospheric movement in smoke, haze, aura fog, or background mist.

Best for:
- forest dream scenes
- occult icons
- mushrooms
- animal posters
- cosmic feminine scenes

Behavior:
- drifting, floating, slight curling
- layered parallax if available
- low opacity movement

Avoid:
- thick smoke that obscures the main subject
- high-speed turbulence

### 3. Star Twinkle
Description:
Sparse brightness variation in stars or sparkles.

Best for:
- outer field decoration
- moon halos
- celestial portraits
- occult frames

Behavior:
- stochastic
- tiny intensity pulses
- low density
- occasional short glints

Avoid:
- equal twinkling on every star
- Christmas-ass blinking chaos

### 4. Chromatic Shimmer
Description:
Very slight color shifting or edge interference in bright glow regions.

Best for:
- eyes
- crystal edges
- magical symbols
- portal rings
- moon rims

Behavior:
- subtle
- restrained
- localized
- slightly spectral

Avoid:
- rainbow flicker across the whole image
- aggressive digital glitching unless hybridized on purpose

### 5. Spore Drift
Description:
Small luminous dots or particles moving upward, outward, or hovering.

Best for:
- mushroom posters
- toxic bio-glow scenes
- portals
- occult fungal hybrids

Behavior:
- slow rise
- varying sizes
- sparse distribution
- occasional pause or drift change

Avoid:
- snowstorm density
- random confetti motion

### 6. Halo Ripple
Description:

Faint rippling through circular or oval aura fields.

Best for:
- portal rings
- moons
- radiant eyes
- occult altars
- concentric symbol assemblies

Behavior:
- subtle radial motion
- low speed
- can be periodic or noise-driven

Avoid:
- loud sonar-wave behavior
- overly visible band stepping

### 7. Hair / Fur Light Crawl
Description:
Very subtle movement of light along hair strands or fur edges rather than literal object motion.

Best for:
- wolves
- big cats
- moon maidens
- glam sorceresses

Behavior:
- edge shimmer
- directional emission crawl
- low intensity, small-area behavior

Avoid:
- making the subject look wet or metallic unless intended

## Recommended Motion Hierarchy

1. Main subject aura motion
2. Atmospheric drift
3. Small accent twinkles
4. Specialized local motion like spores or light-crawl

Do not animate every layer equally.

The focal subject must remain the emotional anchor.

## Speed Guidance

### Very Slow
Use for:
- sacred, dreamy, lunar, romantic pieces

### Slow
Use for:
- most classic blacklight poster motion

### Medium
Use for:
- toxic biofluorescent energy
- more electric 80s hybrids
- portal sequences

### Fast
Rarely use.
Fast motion usually breaks the style unless doing a deliberate hybrid.

## Motion and Era Bias

### 70s-Leaning Motion
- softer
- slower
- dreamier
- more mist and aura breathing
- less edge flicker

### 80s-Leaning Motion
- slightly sharper
- more electric shimmer
- clearer pulse structure
- stronger local emission behavior

## Motion Failure Modes

### Everything Moves
Result:
- visual chaos
- poster identity collapses

### Motion Too Fast
Result:
- loses mystical wall-art feeling
- becomes VJ graphics or EDM sludge

### Motion Too Uniform
Result:
- fake
- dead
- screensaver-ass

### Motion Too Bright
Result:
- destroys black field integrity
- kills the velvet illusion

## Final Rule

The motion should feel like the image is slowly waking up inside the black.
Not dancing for tips under an LED bar.

# Focal Hierarchy

## Purpose

This file defines how focus should be organized in vintage blacklight poster compositions.

This style lives or dies on immediate readability.
The viewer should know what the main thing is from across the room, even if the image
contains decorative madness.

## Core Principle

The composition should have:
- one primary focal subject
- one or two secondary supports
- a decorative field that enhances but does not compete

Think of it like this:

**primary icon**
-> **supporting aura and symbols**
-> **background darkness and decoration**

If everything is equally loud, the image becomes fluorescent bullshit soup.

## Main Hierarchy Levels

### Level 1: Primary Focal Subject
This is the thing the poster is fundamentally about.

Examples:
- wolf face
- moon maiden
- mushroom cluster

- dragon head
- radiant eye
- crystal altar
- moon disc in a landscape variant

Requirements:
- largest or highest-contrast subject
- strongest silhouette
- strongest glow emphasis
- clearest position in the composition
- easiest across-room read

### Level 2: Secondary Support Subjects
These support the main thing emotionally or symbolically.

Examples:
- full moon behind wolf
- crystal clusters beside sorceress
- portal ring behind mushrooms
- moon phases around face
- stars and sigils around an occult eye

Requirements:
- smaller or dimmer than primary
- should reinforce the main subject
- should not compete for equal attention
- should orbit, frame, or anchor the primary

### Level 3: Decorative Field
This is the atmospheric and ornamental layer.

Examples:
- sparkles
- smoke ribbons
- aura rings
- stars
- mist haze
- constellation dots
- spore particles

Requirements:
- lowest hierarchy
- supportive only
- should guide the eye, not hijack it

- should preserve black space between clusters

## Focal Structure Models

### 1. Icon + Halo
Primary subject with one major glow support.

Examples:
- wolf + moon
- eye + aura ring
- sorceress + halo

Use when:
- you want maximum clarity
- you want classic poster immediacy

### 2. Icon + Two Supports
Primary subject with two meaningful reinforcements.

Examples:
- moon maiden + crescents + stars
- mushroom cluster + portal ring + spores
- dragon head + smoke + moon

Use when:
- you want richer storytelling without clutter collapse

### 3. Totemic Stack
Primary focal event stretched vertically with clear internal hierarchy.

Examples:
- moon / face / crystal cluster
- wolf / moon / mountain
- eye / sigil ring / smoke altar

Use when:
- you want ritual or shrine-like presentation

## Hierarchy Tools

### 1. Scale
The main subject should usually be the biggest form.

### 2. Brightness

The brightest localized region should typically align with the focal subject.

### 3. Contrast
The sharpest value and color contrast should happen near the primary focal region.

### 4. Edge Clarity
The cleanest edge behavior belongs to the main subject.

### 5. Centering or Dominant Placement
The primary subject is often centered or given dominant visual weight.

### 6. Glow Priority
The strongest rim glow, halo, or internal radiance belongs to the primary event.

## Black Space as Hierarchy Tool

Darkness is not empty.
Darkness creates attention by isolating the important things.

Use black space to:
- separate the primary from decorative clutter
- give the eye rest
- increase glow punch
- prevent equal-area noise fields

## Common Hierarchy Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Too Many Main Subjects
Problem:
The poster feels confused.

Fix:
Choose one.
The rest must become support.

### Mistake 2: Decorative Hijack
Problem:
Stars, smoke, sparkles, and sigils become louder than the actual subject.

Fix:
Reduce density or brightness of decoration.

### Mistake 3: Equal Glow Everywhere
Problem:

No hierarchy.
Everything screams.

Fix:
Create primary, secondary, and tertiary glow levels.

### Mistake 4: Weak Center
Problem:
The image has atmosphere but no icon.

Fix:
Enlarge or intensify the primary subject.

### Mistake 5: Landscape Overload
Problem:
Environment detail competes with the poster’s core emblematic read.

Fix:
Simplify environment into large supporting forms.

## Suggested Ratios

A good default composition may distribute visual importance roughly like this:

- primary subject: 55–70%
- secondary supports: 20–30%
- decorative field: 10–20%

This is not literal area coverage.
It is attention weight.

## Subgenre Notes

### Cosmic Animal
Primary:
face or upper body icon

Secondary:
moon, mist, stars

### Celestial Feminine
Primary:
face and hair halo

# Negative Space Rules

## Purpose

This file defines how black or near-black negative space should behave in the vintage blacklight
poster style.

Negative space is not leftover emptiness.
It is one of the core engines of the style.

Without preserved darkness:
- fluorescence weakens
- glow hierarchy collapses
- poster readability drops
- the image turns into crowded rainbow mush

## Core Principle

Black space is active.
It amplifies color, frames the focal subject, and creates the velvet-void illusion that makes the
style work.

## Main Negative Space Functions

### 1. Glow Amplifier
Darkness makes bright pigment appear brighter.

### 2. Subject Separator
Black space isolates the main icon from decorative clutter.

### 3. Mood Generator
The void creates mystery, dreaminess, and theatricality.

### 4. Rhythm Control
Empty dark areas give the eye places to rest between glowing events.

### 5. Poster Scale Signal
Strong black space helps the image read like iconic wall art rather than crowded illustration.

## Recommended Darkness Levels

### Classic Poster Mode
- 45–65% black or near-black field

### Dense Maximalist Mode
- 35–50% black or near-black field
- use carefully to avoid clutter collapse

### Dream Landscape Mode
- 40–55% black or near-black field
- more atmosphere allowed but darkness still essential

### Symbolic / Occult Mode
- 50–70% black or near-black field
- strong focus and ritual clarity

## Where to Preserve Darkness

### Around the Main Silhouette
Keep enough black around the primary subject to preserve edge glow clarity.

### In Outer Corners
Corners often benefit from staying darker to hold the composition together.

### Between Decorative Clusters
Do not fill every gap with stars, smoke, mist, symbols, or particles.

### Behind Halo Structures
Even if the halo is bright, surrounding darkness should remain visible.

## Prompt Language for Strong Negative Space

Use phrases like:
- deep black velvet background
- near-total black void
- black-dominant composition
- luminous subject emerging from darkness
- sparse stars against blackness
- floating in black velvet space
- surrounded by deep darkness
- isolated against a black field

## Prompt Language for Moderate Negative Space

Use phrases like:
- black cosmic field with soft aura haze
- dark atmospheric void
- moonlit darkness with sparse glow elements
- mostly black background with drifting mist

## Warning Signs That Negative Space Is Failing

### 1. The Image Feels Crowded
Likely cause:
too many decorative motifs or support elements

### 2. Glow Feels Weak
Likely cause:
not enough contrast between lit and unlit zones

### 3. The Piece Looks Like Generic Psychedelic Wallpaper
Likely cause:
darkness has been replaced by all-over color and motion

### 4. The Poster No Longer Reads From a Distance

Likely cause:
the black field is broken into too many noisy little shapes

## Decorative Density Limits

Decorative elements like:
- stars
- sparkles
- smoke
- spores
- sigils
- radiating bands

should generally appear in clusters, not as equal coverage across the entire image.

Let blackness remain visible between them.

## Near-Black Variants

Pure black is usually best, but near-black hues can be used selectively:
- near-black blue
- near-black violet
- near-black indigo

Use near-black when:
- you want cooler atmosphere
- you want subtle depth
- you want chromatic darkness without losing the blacklight effect

Do not overdo it.
If the whole background turns into dark purple fog, the blacklight force weakens.

## Negative Space by Subgenre

### Cosmic Animal
Use large black fields around the face and moon.

### Celestial Feminine
Keep outer field black even if hair halo and stars are present.

### Mushroom Vision
Preserve black under and around the mushroom cluster so spores and bloom pop.

### Occult Symbol

Use large black void around the core symbol for ritual intensity.

### Fantasy Landscape
Keep details simplified and preserve large dark sky or foreground regions.

## Final Rule

The darkness should feel like velvet abyss, not unused background.
If the black isn’t doing work, it isn’t enough black.

## Purpose

This file defines example render pipelines for translating the vintage blacklight poster style into
shaders, node graphs, compositing systems, realtime rendering, or post-processing stacks.

These are not strict technical specs.
They are visual logic recipes meant to preserve the blacklight poster identity.

Build the image in layers.
Do not try to brute-force the whole look with one giant bloom pass and a prayer.

The style depends on:
- silhouette clarity
- localized emission
- layered glow
- preserved black field
- selective decorative detail

---

- wolf
- tiger

- panther
- owl
- eagle

  - optional faint mist in outer field

  - keep subject readable before adding glow

  - portal

  - layered haze
  - subtle color banding
  - soft clouds or fog

5. **Decorative Pass**
  - sparse stars

  - simplify edges

A blacklight poster should look like a luminous icon emerging from darkness in controlled

Not like the whole framebuffer caught a mystical STD.

# Subject Anatomy Biases

## Purpose

This file defines how different subject families should be stylized anatomically within the vintage
1970s–1980s blacklight poster aesthetic.

This is not realism anatomy.
This is poster anatomy.

The point is not to render subjects accurately in a scientific sense.
The point is to exaggerate, simplify, glamorize, or mythologize them so they read strongly as
glowing icons against darkness.

## Core Principle

In this style, anatomy should be:
- simplified
- readable
- emotionally charged
- edge-friendly
- silhouette-friendly
- glow-compatible

Avoid overly subtle naturalistic structure that disappears in blacklight conditions.

## Global Anatomy Rules

1. Favor iconic silhouettes over complex realism.
2. Emphasize eyes, hair, fur, cap rims, horns, crystals, and contour lines.
3. Simplify small forms into larger readable masses.
4. Use anatomy to support glow zones.
5. Exaggerate what makes the subject emotionally legible.

## Subject Family Biases

---

## 1. Wolves

### Anatomy Bias
Wolves should feel noble, spectral, and emotionally direct.

### Emphasize
- large face shape
- strong eye region
- ear silhouette
- cheek fur flares

- neck ruff
- muzzle contour

### Reduce
- tiny fur realism
- overly anatomical jaw detail
- fussy teeth rendering

### Glow Targets
- eyes
- ear edges
- cheek fur edges
- moon-facing contours
- nose bridge highlights

### Poster Logic
The wolf should read instantly as a large symbolic face in darkness.

---

## 2. Tigers / Big Cats

### Anatomy Bias
Big cats should feel sleek, feral, glam, and high-contrast.

### Emphasize
- eye slant
- stripe rhythm
- whisker pad
- cheekbone contour
- ear tips
- sharp muzzle silhouette

### Reduce
- overly realistic fur breakup
- naturalistic body complexity if face-focused

### Glow Targets
- stripe edges
- eyes
- whiskers
- jaw contour
- forehead planes

### Poster Logic
The face should feel like an explosive mask of orange heat and cool shadow.

---

## 3. Panthers

### Anatomy Bias
Panthers should feel elegant, dangerous, and void-born.

### Emphasize
- sleek head shape
- glowing eyes
- minimal but clean facial contour
- long whisker arcs
- shoulder line if visible

### Reduce
- internal body clutter
- excessive texture

### Glow Targets
- whiskers
- eye rims
- back contour
- cheek and forehead edge light

### Poster Logic
The power comes from black-on-black subtlety plus selective electric separation.

---

## 4. Owls / Birds

### Anatomy Bias
Owls should feel luminous, sacred, nocturnal, and watchful.

### Emphasize
- eye disks
- frontal face symmetry
- beak center
- feather mass shape
- wing edge if visible

### Reduce
- too much feather realism
- tiny feather pattern noise

### Glow Targets
- eyes
- feather edge halos
- moonlit head outline
- chest plumage highlights

### Poster Logic
Owls work best when they feel like floating ritual masks.

---

## 5. Celestial Feminine Faces

### Anatomy Bias

### Emphasize
- eye shape
- cheekbone planes
- lips
- brow arc
- hair mass
- face oval
- neck and shoulder arc

### Reduce
- pore realism
- hyper-photographic anatomy
- modern beauty-editorial sharpness
- subtle natural shadow logic

### Glow Targets
- eye accents
- cheek glow
- brow highlights
- lip edge gleam
- hair halo
- collarbone shimmer if present

### Poster Logic

The face is a moon-symbol, not a candid person.

### Emphasize

- hands if used for spell framing
- hair plume volume
- crystal-adjacent contours
- jewelry / adornment edges

### Reduce
- body realism
- overly specific costume folds unless they contribute to silhouette

### Glow Targets
- fingers
- eyes
- hair halo
- jewelry
- magical object edges

### Poster Logic
The body should support the face and aura, not compete with them.

- gill rhythm

- silhouette separation between caps

- muddy cap textures

### Glow Targets
- cap rims
- gill interiors
- spore zones
- stem undersides
- portal-facing edges

### Poster Logic
The mushroom cluster should behave almost like a cathedral, idol, or shrine.

### Anatomy Bias
These should feel geometric but still glow-organic.

### Emphasize
- central axis
- major facets
- core radiance
- outer contour
- point directions

### Reduce
- too many micro-facets
- sterile CAD-like geometry

### Glow Targets
- inner core
- facet edges
- tip highlights
- aura field around structure

### Poster Logic
The object should feel ritual-charged, not technically modeled.

---

## 9. Dragons / Fantasy Beasts

### Anatomy Bias
Fantasy beasts should feel theatrical and emblematic, not zoologically overexplained.

### Emphasize
- head silhouette
- eye
- horn shape
- jaw contour
- crest or mane
- smoke-breath pathways

### Reduce
- cluttered scales
- too many anatomical systems competing at once

### Glow Targets
- eye
- horn edges
- mouth interior
- crest
- smoke edges

### Poster Logic
The beast should read like a myth signal, not a monster manual page.

---

## 10. Landscapes

### Anatomy Bias
Landscape forms should be broad and symbolic.

### Emphasize
- moon circle
- waterfall verticals
- mountain triangles
- portal rings
- tree silhouettes
- horizon glow bands

### Reduce
- small terrain detail

- realistic foliage density
- excessive depth realism

### Glow Targets
- focal waterfall core
- moon edge
- crystal peaks
- fog pockets
- portal openings

### Poster Logic
Landscape anatomy should still support a poster icon, not become fussy illustration.

## Face Bias Rules

For face-based posters:
- enlarge eyes slightly
- smooth or simplify transitions
- keep face shape readable from a distance
- let hair and halo function as secondary framing shapes
- use symmetry loosely, not mechanically

## Body Bias Rules

When a body is visible:
- simplify pose into clear silhouette
- emphasize shoulders, neck, arm arcs, or hand gestures
- avoid busy limb overlap
- keep glow behavior readable

## Final Rule

The subject should feel more archetypal than literal.
If anatomy becomes too ordinary, the poster loses its spell.

## Usage Notes

To push a set more 70s:
- increase painterly softness
- reduce edge sharpness
- add dream haze
- emphasize mystical analog mood

To push a set more 80s:
- sharpen contour glow slightly
- increase cyan-magenta clarity
- use slightly punchier contrast
- add stronger theatrical emission

To keep results on-style:
- preserve black space
- do not let decoration compete equally with the main icon
- reserve white for peak energy only

# Era Split Rules

## Purpose

This file defines how to distinguish late-1970s blacklight poster tendencies from early-1980s
blacklight poster tendencies, plus how to build a balanced hybrid.

This is not museum-ass art history.
It is practical style control.

Use this file when you want to bias a prompt, shader, or design toward:
- softer 70s mystical poster logic
- sharper 80s electric fantasy logic
- a blended 70s/80s blacklight mode

## Core Principle

Both eras share the main blacklight anchors:
- black-dominant background
- fluorescent palette logic
- glow hierarchy
- iconic wall-poster composition
- fantasy / mystical / cosmic / animal symbolism

The difference is mainly in:
- softness vs sharpness
- mystical haze vs electric clarity
- symbolic dreaminess vs punchy fantasy charge

---

## Late 1970s Bias

### Core Feel
- softer
- dreamier
- more mystical
- more analog
- more hazy and plush
- more spiritual / head-shop / zodiac-adjacent

### Palette Tendencies
- violet
- magenta
- deep blue
- cyan
- warmer analog orange used softly
- less brutally sharp cyan-magenta splitting

### Glow Tendencies
- broader soft halos
- more haze
- more velvety transitions
- less crisp edge snap
- more dreamy aura wrapping

### Composition Tendencies
- more symbolic softness
- gentler framing
- slower visual rhythm
- a little less aggressive contrast in edge treatment

### Motif Tendencies
- zodiac
- moons
- dream women
- wolves
- waterfalls
- mushrooms
- cosmic clouds
- stars
- occult softness rather than hard ritual precision

### Texture Tendencies
- more airbrush mist
- more overspray feel

- more plush black
- more analog softness

### Motion Tendencies
- slower aura breathing
- mist drift
- gentle moon pulse
- softer star twinkle

### Risks
- too soft = weak poster punch
- too pastel = loses blacklight force
- too decorative = greeting-card or generic mystic drift

---

## Early 1980s Bias

### Core Feel
- more electric
- more dramatic
- slightly cleaner
- punchier contrast
- more fantasy-paperback adjacent
- more theatrical

### Palette Tendencies
- stronger electric blue
- clearer magenta/cyan interplay
- hotter orange and yellow accents
- more aggressive contrast splits

### Glow Tendencies
- stronger rim emission
- slightly tighter bloom
- more noticeable highlight snap
- more controlled edge definition

### Composition Tendencies
- stronger focal locking
- clearer center emphasis
- more theatrical subject impact
- slightly more dramatic silhouette use

### Motif Tendencies
- dragons
- fantasy beasts
- sorceresses
- dramatic big cats
- portal-like glow structures
- bolder moon halos
- stronger electric ornament

### Texture Tendencies
- cleaner airbrush
- slightly less haze
- stronger focal emphasis
- less diffuse softness overall

### Motion Tendencies
- slightly sharper local shimmer
- stronger portal pulse
- more defined edge crawl
- still not fast, just more electrically articulate

### Risks
- too crisp = drifts digital
- too chrome-adjacent = drifts van art or synth hybrid
- too aggressive = loses dreaminess

---

## Hybrid 70s/80s Mode

### Core Feel
This is usually the best default:
- mystical softness of the 70s
- electric punch of the 80s
- painterly glow with strong focal clarity

### Recommended Balance
- 70s for atmosphere, softness, black velvet mood
- 80s for focal hierarchy, emission clarity, and theatrical punch

### Best Use Cases
- wolves
- moon maidens
- sorceresses

- mushrooms
- crystal altars
- dragon heads
- owl posters
- shrine-like compositions

### Visual Formula
- soft atmospheric black field
- strong central icon
- layered glow hierarchy
- controlled bright accents
- dreamy but legible composition

---

## Prompt Bias Language

### To Push More 70s
Use phrases like:
- softer late-1970s blacklight poster feel
- mystical head-shop wall-poster atmosphere
- dreamy analog airbrush softness
- velvety haze and aura
- softer spiritual glow logic

### To Push More 80s
Use phrases like:
- early-1980s blacklight fantasy poster energy
- cleaner electric airbrush glow
- stronger cyan-magenta clarity
- punchier theatrical focal emphasis
- sharper fantasy poster silhouette

### To Stay Hybrid
Use phrases like:
- vintage 1970s–1980s blacklight poster
- dreamy airbrush glow with strong focal clarity
- velvety mystical darkness and electric emission
- classic blacklight poster fantasy energy

---

## Knob Guidance

### More 70s
- increase softness
- increase mystical density
- reduce edge sharpness
- reduce highlight harshness
- keep motion slower
- allow more aura haze

### More 80s
- increase glow aggression slightly
- tighten bloom
- increase rim clarity
- sharpen focal edges
- use stronger hot/cool contrast
- keep decorative clutter controlled

---

## Final Rule

If the piece feels like a dreamy fluorescent vision glowing in black velvet, you’ve kept the 70s.
If it also slaps with dramatic focal punch, you’ve brought in the 80s.
That hybrid zone is the sweet spot.
