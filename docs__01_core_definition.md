# Core Definition

## Definition

Vintage 1970s–1980s blacklight poster style is a fluorescence-driven visual language designed
to appear electrically luminous under ultraviolet light or to simulate that effect in normal viewing
conditions.

It relies on extreme contrast between dark negative space and aggressively saturated color
fields, often arranged around mystical, cosmic, fantasy, erotic, animal, or symbolic subjects.

The style is less concerned with realism than with visual impact, psychic charge, and
wall-presence.

## Essential Identity

At its core, this style is:

- black-dominant
- glow-dominant
- icon-driven
- mood-heavy
- painterly or airbrushed
- symbolic rather than documentary
- theatrical rather than naturalistic

## Historical Flavor

This visual language emerged in relation to:

- blacklight-reactive poster printing
- head shops and novelty stores
- dorm-room and basement wall decor
- fantasy illustration culture
- van art and airbrush culture
- soft erotica / mystical femininity imagery
- space, zodiac, mushrooms, big cats, wolves, and celestial scenes

## Primary Viewing Effect

The image should feel like it is doing one or more of the following:

- glowing from inside the blackness
- hovering in void-space
- radiating aura or field energy
- presenting an emblematic fantasy icon
- functioning as ritual decor, bedroom theater, or dream bait

## Style Principle

The black background is not empty.

It is the stage, the abyss, the velvet, and the contrast engine that makes all fluorescence
possible.

Without darkness, the style dies.
